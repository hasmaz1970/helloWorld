    Principles and Concepts

    This section details the architectural principles being general rules and guidelines, intended to be enduring and seldom amended, that relate to design of MonE.

    It shall be noted that the approach taken here is aligned to TOGAF and TMFORUM Open Digital Architecture (ODA) standards. The following sections provide insights to these principles. For further details, please see Ref. [4], [5].

    In this regard, these architecture principles govern the architecture process, affecting the development, maintenance, and use of the enterprise architecture.

    The principles and are split into the following sectors:

    Sector Description
    1. General Principles Overarching but general principles that are common for all sectors.
    2. Business Principles Principles that guide alignment with organizational units. These principles embody the health and success of the organization as a whole.
    3. Information & Data Principles Provides guidance on information and data use and the management of both structured and unstructured data by drawing on local and regional strategies, best practices and regulatory regimes.
    4. Application Principles Non technology-based application component impacting principles to decouple the organization from vendor specific limitations or proprietary approaches to solutions.
    5. Technology and Component Principles Ensures that components developed in different or for different execution functional blocks or environments are interoperable across functional blocks.


    1. General Principles

    • Different stakeholders, different viewpoints
    • Technology functions must operate as businesses
    • Think out of the box
    • Stepwise evolution to target / Walk, Crawl, Run
    • Governance is integrated: Information should be captured electronically once, and immediately validated as close to the source as possible. Quality control measures must be implemented to ensure the integrity of information.

    2. Business Principles

    • Primacy of Principles: These principles of information management apply to all organizations within the enterprise. The only way we can provide a consistent and measurable level of quality information to decision-makers is if all organizations abide by the principles.
    • Maximize Benefit to the Enterprise: Information management decisions are made to provide maximum benefit to the enterprise as a whole.
    • Information Management is Everybody's Business: All organizations in the enterprise participate in information management decisions needed to accomplish business objectives. In order to ensure information management is aligned with the business, all organizations in the enterprise must be involved in all aspects of the information environment. The business experts from across the enterprise and the technical staff responsible for developing and sustaining the information environment need to come together as a team to jointly define the goals and objectives of IT.
    • Business Continuity: Enterprise operations are maintained in spite of system interruptions. Dependency on shared system applications mandates that the risks of business interruption must be established in advance and managed.
    • Reusable and Common Use Application Components: Development of application components used across the enterprise is preferred over the development of similar or duplicative applications which are only provided to a particular organization.
    • Service Orientation: Service orientation delivers enterprise agility and no-boundary Information Flow.. The architecture is based on a design of services which mirror real-world business activities comprising the enterprise (or inter-enterprise) business processes.
    • Compliance with Law: Enterprise information management processes comply with all relevant laws, policies, and regulations.

    3. Information and Data Principles

    • Common Vocabulary and Definitions: Data is defined consistently throughout the enterprise, and the definitions are understandable and available to all users.
    • Data is a shared asset: Data is an asset that has value to the enterprise and is managed accordingly. Users have access to the data necessary to perform their duties; therefore, data is shared across enterprise functions and organizations.
    • Data is accessible: Data is accessible for users and systems to perform their functions.
    • Data must be secure: Data is protected from unauthorized use and disclosure. In addition to the traditional aspects of national security classification, this includes, but is not limited to, protection of pre-decisional, sensitive, source selection-sensitive, and proprietary information.
    • Data Trustee and Ownership (Stewardship): A trustee is different than steward/owner. As the degree of data sharing grows and business units rely upon common information, it becomes critical that only trustee (having overall view) makes decisions about the content and lifecycle of the data even if data is owned by a different party. Thus, a trustee is responsible for accuracy and currency of the data.
    • Data that can be analyzed: Information assets provide invaluable information to the enterprise for research, business intelligence decision-making and operational intelligence when gathered, stored and accessed correctly. So, analytics viewpoint must be considered while managing data operationally. If required relevant notification/ data feed channels must be considered within the architecture for or business and operational analytics purposes.

    4. Application Component Principles

    • Ease of use: Application components are easy to use. The underlying technology is transparent to users, so they can concentrate on tasks at hand.
    • Technology Agnostic: Application components are independent of specific technology choices and therefore can operate on a variety of technology platforms. Realizing that every decision made with respect to IT makes us dependent on that technology, the intent of this principle is to ensure that Application Software is not dependent on specific/proprietary hardware, operating system, middleware or platform software.
    • Modular and Reusable Packaging: Leverage industry standards and guidelines for modularization and reusability. Prevent application redundancy.
    • Decoupled: Components are designed to be decoupled and are autonomous and unaware of each other.
    • Location Independent Capability Exposure: Components will interact with data, technology and other applications. Exposing capabilities as services based on well-defined intent, standards and open interfaces will drive connected business value.

    5. Technology and Component Principles

    5.1. General Technology Principles

    This section is the TOGAF compliant technology principles extended and to be considered.

    • Requirements-Based Change: Only in response to business needs are changes to applications and technology made. This principle will foster an atmosphere where the information environment changes in response to the needs of the business, rather than having the business change in response to IT changes.
    • Responsive Change Management: Processes for managing and implementing change must be developed that do not create delays. Managing and implementing change is not enough unless coupled with automated deployment and go-live procedures.
    • Control and Manage Technical Diversity: Technological diversity is controlled to minimize the non-trivial cost of maintaining expertise in and connectivity between multiple processing environments. Technology decisions are guided by a holistic Enterprise Architecture and a standards conformant technological blueprint governed/advised by relevant subject matter expert organizations such as: TMFORUM, TOGAF, ETSI, 3GPP, IETF, Broadband Forum, etc.
    • Interoperability: Software and hardware should conform to defined standards that promote interoperability for data, applications, and technology.

    5.2. Component Principles

    This section is the TOGAF compliant technology principles extended and to be considered.

    • Components expose catalog enabled, discoverable and location transparent capabilities: In a services/capability driven modular and distributed enterprise architecture, the number of capabilities to be dealt with is quite high. And also, their locations change dynamically owing to the dynamically scalable and agile nature of these modules. Therefore, at least one or more of the following design options shall be considered part of the design. (Please also see Note1 for additional information below):

      • Any access to a capability or service between components or from external clients will be via a dynamic URI based look-up and resolution or via some service/capability discovery procedure.
      • Each component may also register its capabilities/services in a catalog on startup and deregister on shutdown with additional mechanisms of heartbeat, allowing proper housekeeping in case of unplanned downtimes or network/interconnectivity issues.

    • Components are decoupled and segregated: Designed to be autonomous and unaware of each other. Each application NOT only is designed to perform its tasks independently - but also allows components to remain completely autonomous and unaware of each other. Meaning sub components may be deployed, upgraded independently.

    • Components are monitorable and analyzable: Components emit applicational LOGs as well as transactional KPI information for analytical systems (like average latency, average throughput, CPU/IO utilization etc.)

    • Components are functional LEGO blocks: Modules/components can provide one or more discrete set of APIs resulting in a discrete functional capability block fostering coherent architecture with easy swap-in/out. Therefore, in this regards, technologies and approaches that favor platform decoupling, as well as scalability decoupling are to be favored.

    Note1

    • Location independence at network address level: DNS based lookups provide location independence at network address level based on a URIs.
    • Location independence at URI level: At URI level, more particularly, the URL being a specialization of URI, the network services are typically referenced by URLs. The name URL (Universal Resource Locator) applies to a particular object/service and the location to get it. Therefore, if the object/document service is available/served by and in multiple servers/locations, this results in the client NOT to be location independent. This was one of the reasons why IETF has defined URN (Universal Resource Name) such that access to URN returns a dynamic list of URLs which following, the client/browser may use one of these returned URLs to access the document/service.

    Considering location independency with load-balancing, MonE components prefer Server Side approach capabilities provided out-of-the-box with Container Orchestration Platforms such as Kubernetes or OpenShift.

    • In Server-Side discovery mechanisms client invokes the API reference exposed by a server side component (Load Balancer). That component calls the service registry and determines the absolute location of the service/capability.
    • In Client-Side discovery mechanisms Client ( or with the help of an API Gateway) obtains and decides on the actual service to be invoked upon querying service registry.
